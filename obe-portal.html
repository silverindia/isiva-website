1.1 What is OBE?

Outcomes-Based Education is a learner-centred philosophy where curriculum, teaching, and assessment are all aligned to clearly defined, measurable outcomes students must demonstrate by programme completion. The shift is from what is taught to what is actually learned and demonstrated.

Core Principle: An 'outcome' is a specific, observable, measurable statement of what a student will know, understand, or be able to do at the end of a unit of learning — written in terms of student performance, not teacher activity.

1.2 Bloom's Taxonomy

All Course Outcomes must be anchored to a specific cognitive level using Bloom's revised taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001):

LevelCategoryKey Action Verbs
6CreateDesign, Develop, Formulate, Construct, Plan, Generate
5EvaluateJudge, Justify, Critique, Assess, Recommend, Defend
4AnalyseCompare, Differentiate, Examine, Break down, Relate
3ApplySolve, Use, Demonstrate, Compute, Implement, Calculate
2UnderstandExplain, Describe, Summarise, Classify, Interpret
1RememberDefine, List, Name, Recall, State, Identify
NBA Guideline: UG engineering COs should predominantly be at Levels 3 (Apply) and 4 (Analyse). Design courses must include Levels 5–6.

Bloom's Revised Taxonomy

Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) revised Bloom's original framework into six cognitive levels — from basic recall at the base to complex creation at the apex. Every CO must be anchored to one specific level using an action verb from that tier.

Create / Evaluate — higher-order, complex
Analyse / Apply — NBA core for UG engineering
Understand / Remember — foundational
For NBA UG engineering, most COs should be at Level 3 (Apply) and Level 4 (Analyse). Design courses must include at least 2 COs at Levels 5–6.

Institutional Vision & Mission
Why the institution exists — the foundation of everything
L0 ▾
Broad aspirational statements that define the institution's purpose and long-term direction. Every element in OBE — from PEOs down to COs — must be traceable back to the Vision and Mission.

Vision eg: "To be a globally recognised institution producing innovative, ethical, and socially responsible engineers."
Mission eg: "To impart quality technical education through outcome-based learning, research, and industry collaboration."

Reviewed every 5 yearsGoverning Body approvalNAAC Criterion 1.1

Programme Educational Objectives (PEOs)
Graduate accomplishments 3–5 years after graduation
L1 ▾
Broad statements describing career and professional accomplishments expected 3–5 years after graduation. Validated by alumni and employers. Typically 4–6 PEOs per programme.

PEO1: Graduates will apply engineering knowledge to design and develop solutions to complex problems in industry or research.
PEO2: Graduates will demonstrate professional integrity and effective communication in multidisciplinary environments.
PEO3: Graduates will pursue continuous learning through higher education or professional development.

Programme Outcomes (POs) + PSOs
12 NBA-mandated POs — what students can do at graduation
L2 ▾
NBA mandates all 12 POs for UG engineering. PSOs (2–3) capture what is unique to the specific discipline. PO attainment is computed by aggregating CO attainment across all courses.

PO1 Engineering Knowledge  |  PO2 Problem Analysis  |  PO3 Design/Development  |  PO4 Investigation  |  PO5 Modern Tool Usage  |  PO6 Engineer & Society  |  PO7 Environment  |  PO8 Ethics  |  PO9 Team Work  |  PO10 Communication  |  PO11 Project Management  |  PO12 Life-Long Learning

📋
Course Outcomes (COs)
5–8 per course — measurable, Bloom's-anchored, maps to POs
L3 ▾
Specific, observable, measurable statements of what a student will be able to do at the end of a course. Each CO: one Bloom's verb + knowledge/skill + context/standard. Maps to POs using strength 1/2/3.

CO1 [BL2]: Explain the laws of thermodynamics and their applications in engineering systems.
CO3 [BL3]: Apply Bernoulli's equation to solve problems in pipe flow and flow measurement devices.
CO5 [BL5]: Evaluate and recommend optimal heat exchanger designs for given industrial thermal specifications.

5–8 per courseOne Bloom's verb eachNBA SAR 2.2–2.4
Design down, deliver up: Design the curriculum starting from Vision and working downward. When teaching, start from COs and build upward — CO attainment → PO attainment → PEO validation → Mission fulfilled.

2.1 Anatomy of a Well-Written CO

Every well-written CO has three parts:

ElementWhat it meansExample
Action verbSingle Bloom's verb — observable and measurableDesign
Knowledge/skillSpecific content or skill demonstrateda heat exchanger for given thermal specifications
Context/standardConditions or performance standardusing IS standards and sustainability considerations
Well-written CO example: "Design a heat exchanger for given thermal specifications using relevant IS standards and sustainability considerations." [BL5, maps to PO1, PO3, PO7]

2.2 Common CO Writing Mistakes

MistakeWrongCorrect
Vague verbUnderstand thermodynamicsExplain the laws of thermodynamics and their engineering applications
Multi-verb COAnalyse and design and evaluate circuitsSplit into three COs — one verb each
Topic statementUnit 3: Fluid MechanicsApply Bernoulli's equation to solve problems in pipe flow
UnmeasurableAppreciate the importance of ethicsApply ethical frameworks to resolve professional dilemmas
Too many COs15 COs for a 3-credit course5–8 COs; one per 5–8 contact hours

2.3 CO–PO Mapping Strength Scale

StrengthMeaningWhen to use
3SubstantialThe CO directly and substantially addresses the PO — primary alignment
2ModerateThe PO is partially addressed as part of achieving the CO
1SlightIndirect or minor relevance; marginal connection
—NoneNo meaningful relationship — leave cell blank
Mapping guidance: A typical course addresses 6–9 POs meaningfully. Each CO should have 3–6 non-blank mappings. Do not try to map every CO to every PO — this inflates the matrix and makes it meaningless.

2.4 CO Approval Workflow

  • Faculty writes draft COs using the template
  • Department CO-PO Review Committee (HOD + 2 senior faculty) reviews for Bloom's alignment
  • IQAC checks for programme-level consistency and NBA/NAAC compliance
  • Approved COs are entered into the course file and LMS
  • Any mid-semester revision requires written IQAC approval

3.2 CO Attainment Formula

CO Attainment = (Direct Attainment × 0.80) + (Indirect Attainment × 0.20)

Direct attainment = % of students scoring ≥ threshold marks in CO-mapped assessments
Indirect attainment = Average score from course-end student feedback survey (CO-wise)
Typical threshold: ≥ 50% of CO-mapped marks
Typical target: ≥ 60% of students should attain each CO

3.3 Worked Example

StepCalculation
CO-mapped marks (3 CIE questions)5 + 5 + 10 = 20 marks
Threshold (50%)≥ 10 marks
Students scoring ≥ 1052 out of 70
Direct attainment(52/70) × 100 = 74.3%
Indirect attainment (survey)68%
CO2 Attainment(74.3 × 0.80) + (68 × 0.20) = 73.0% → ATTAINED ✓

4.1 Constructive Alignment

Every CIE/SEE question must be aligned to the CO it tests, AND the cognitive demand of the question must match the Bloom's level of that CO. Misalignment — writing a CO at Level 5 but testing only Level 1 — invalidates the entire OBE process.

The rule: If your CO says "Design…" (Level 6), your assessment question must ask the student to actually design something — not describe or recall what design means.

4.2 CIE Question Tagging Format (NBA Required)

Q1. [CO1 – BL2] Describe the stress–strain relationship in ductile materials. (5 marks)
Q2. [CO2 – BL3] Calculate the bending stress in a beam carrying 10 kN/m UDL over 4m span. (10 marks)
Q3. [CO3 – BL4] Compare failure modes of ductile and brittle materials under tensile loading. (10 marks)

4.3 Rubric Design for CO Attainment

LevelScoreDescriptor
Excellent4Fully demonstrates the CO; exceeds expected standard with no significant errors
Good3Demonstrates the CO with minor errors; meets expected standard
Satisfactory2Partially demonstrates the CO; some significant gaps

4.4 Corrective Action by Attainment Level

AttainmentAction
≥ Target (e.g., ≥60%)Document "CO attained" in course file — no action needed
50–59%Conduct one additional tutorial/problem-solving session; re-assess informally

5.1 CO Attainment Report — Required Sections

  1. Course details (name, code, credits, semester, faculty, year)
  2. Course Outcomes with Bloom's level
  3. CO-PO/PSO mapping matrix
  4. Assessment plan — CO-wise marks allocation
  5. Mark entry summary (CO-wise per student)
  6. Direct attainment calculation with threshold
  7. Indirect attainment (course-end survey results)
  8. Overall CO attainment and verdict (attained / not attained)
  9. Gap analysis and corrective actions
  10. Faculty signature + HOD countersignature

5.2 NAAC SSR — OBE Evidence by Sub-criterion

NAAC Sub-criterionOBE Evidence Required
1.1 — Curriculum designCO statements in syllabi; BoS minutes showing CO review
1.2 — Academic flexibilityCurriculum updates driven by CO attainment gap analysis
2.6 — Student performanceCO and PO attainment data; CO-mapped assessment samples
6.5 — Quality assuranceIQAC minutes; OBE policy; faculty training records

5.3 CQI — Closing the Loop

  1. Identify COs consistently not attained (2+ years)
  2. Root cause: poorly written CO? wrong teaching method? misaligned assessment?
  3. Implement change: revise CO, change teaching, redesign assessment
  4. Present improvement plan to Board of Studies
  5. Document explicitly — which CO attainment data triggered the change
  6. Monitor in the next offering — verify improvement
The accreditation mantra: Accreditors do not just want high numbers. They want evidence that you act on the data. A course with 55% attainment that has documented corrective action and shows improvement next year is more compelling than one claiming 90% with no supporting evidence.
